AI-Based Protein Complex Prediction with AlphaFold3

BIO512 Structural Biology - Predicting Mutation Effects on Protein Complexes

Author

Sayyed Jalil Mahdizadeh

Published

November 10, 2025

1 Introduction

In this practical, we will apply AI-based protein complex structure prediction using AlphaFold3 (AF3). You will learn about the capabilities of AF3 in predicting protein complexes from wild-type sequences and the effects of mutations on complex formation.

Building on the previous practical where you analyzed the crystal structure of the Barnase-Barstar complex and identified key interacting residues (ASP39 and ASP35), you will now use AF3 to predict how mutations to these critical residues affect complex stability and formation.

Prerequisites
  • Completed analysis of 1BRS crystal structure from previous practical
  • FASTA sequences of Barnase and Barstar from PDB
  • Gmail account for AlphaFold server access
Learning Objectives

By the end you should be able to:

  • Use AlphaFold3 server for protein complex prediction
  • Interpret AF3 confidence metrics (pTM and ipTM)
  • Design and analyze mutational studies computationally
  • Compare predicted structures with experimental crystal structures
  • Understand limitations of AI-based structure prediction for mutations

2 AlphaFold3 Prediction Setup

2.1 Preparing sequences

You should have obtained the FASTA sequences from the previous practical. If not:

  1. Get the sequences:
    • Open www.rcsb.org and search for 1BRS
    • Click on ‘Download Files’ and choose ‘FASTA Sequence’
    • Open the downloaded FASTA file with a text editor
    • You’ll see sequences for both Barnase and Barstar

2.2 Wild-type complex prediction

  1. Access AlphaFold3 server:
    • Navigate to https://alphafoldserver.com
    • Log in with your Gmail account
  2. Submit wild-type prediction:
    • Enter the WT sequence of Barstar (first protein)
    • Add entity as “protein”
    • Enter the WT sequence of Barnase (second protein)
    • Add entity as “protein”
    • Click “Continue and preview job”
    • Give a name to the job: wtBarnase-wtBarstar
    • Submit the job
Processing time

AF3 jobs can take several hours to complete. You may need to check back later or use pre-computed results if available.

  1. Download results:
    • Once completed, click on the job
    • Note the confidence metrics:
      • pTM (global metric): Confidence in individual protein structures
      • ipTM (interface metric): Confidence in protein-protein interface
    • Download results by clicking “Download”
    • Rename the file to wtBarnase_wtBarstar.zip
    • Extract: unzip wtBarnase_wtBarstar.zip -d wtBarnase_wtBarstar

3 Mutational Analysis

Based on the previous structural analysis, we identified that ASP39 and ASP35 of Barstar are critical for binding. We’ll test two different mutation scenarios:

3.1 Mutation to Alanine (conservative)

  1. Prepare D39A-D35A mutant sequences:
    • Take the Barstar sequence from the FASTA file
    • Find position 39 (ASP/D) and replace with Alanine (A)
    • Find position 35 (ASP/D) and replace with Alanine (A)
    • Keep Barnase sequence unchanged
  2. Submit D39A-D35A prediction:
    • Enter the mutated Barstar sequence
    • Enter the WT Barnase sequence
    • Job name: wtBarnase_D39A-D35ABarstar
    • Submit and download results when complete

3.2 Mutation to Arginine (charge reversal)

  1. Prepare D39R-D35R mutant sequences:
    • Take the Barstar sequence from the FASTA file
    • Find position 39 (ASP/D) and replace with Arginine (R)
    • Find position 35 (ASP/D) and replace with Arginine (R)
    • Keep Barnase sequence unchanged
  2. Submit D39R-D35R prediction:
    • Enter the mutated Barstar sequence
    • Enter the WT Barnase sequence
    • Job name: wtBarnase_D39R-D35RBarstar
    • Submit and download results when complete

4 Results Analysis

4.1 Confidence metrics comparison

  1. Compare AF3 confidence scores:
Complex ipTM pTM
WT 0.93 0.94
D39A-D35A 0.89 0.92
D39R-D35R 0.25 0.62
Interpreting metrics
  • pTM: Confidence in overall fold (0-1 scale, >0.8 is high confidence)
  • ipTM: Confidence in interface prediction (0-1 scale, >0.8 is high confidence)
  • Lower scores indicate less confidence in the predicted structure

4.2 Structural comparison with crystal structure

  1. Prepare YASARA for comparison:
    • Clean up YASARA screen: File > New > Yes
    • Open the prepared crystal structure: 1BRS_complex.pdb
    • Open the AF3 predicted WT structure: model_0.cif (from wtBarnase_wtBarstar folder)
AF3 output format

AF3 predicts 5 models (model_0 to model_4) where model_0 always has the highest-ranking confidence value.

  1. Superpose predicted on crystal structure:
    • Analyze > Superpose > Objects
    • Select “fold_wtbaranase…” (predicted structure) from Sequence panel > OK
    • Select “1BRS_complex…” (crystal structure) from Sequence panel > OK
    • In “Set parameters” window: Uncheck all boxes except “Residue name” and “Residue number” > OK
  2. Calculate RMSD for Barstar:
    • Analyze > RMSD of > Molecules
    • Choose “D” (Barstar of crystal structure) from Sequence panel > OK
    • Choose “A” (predicted Barstar) > OK > OK
    • RMSD value will be shown in the command window
  3. Repeat for mutant structures:
    • Clean up YASARA screen and repeat steps 10-12 for both mutant predictions
    • Complete the RMSD comparison table:
Comparison Pair RMSD (Å)
Crystal vs WT 2.76
Crystal vs D39A-D35A 2.90
Crystal vs D39R-D35R 9.42

5 Interpretation and Discussion

5.1 Understanding the results

  1. Analysis of AF3 performance:

The results reveal important insights about AF3’s capabilities and limitations:

5.1.1 Alanine mutations (D39A-D35A):

  • Small decrease in confidence metrics (ipTM: 0.93→0.89)
  • Minimal structural change (RMSD: 2.76→2.90 Å)
  • AF3 prediction: Minor effect on complex formation
  • Experimental reality: These mutations drastically reduce binding affinity

5.1.2 Arginine mutations (D39R-D35R):

  • Dramatic decrease in confidence metrics (ipTM: 0.93→0.25)
  • Large structural deviation (RMSD: 2.76→9.42 Å)
  • AF3 prediction: Major disruption of complex formation
  • Experimental expectation: Strong electrostatic repulsion would indeed disrupt binding

5.2 Critical analysis questions

Answer the following questions based on your results:

5.2.1 AF3 Performance Assessment

  1. How well does AF3 predict the wild-type complex? Compare the confidence metrics and RMSD with the crystal structure.

  2. What do the confidence metrics tell us about AF3’s certainty in its predictions for each variant?

  3. Why might AF3 fail to capture the effect of alanine mutations but successfully predict the impact of arginine mutations?

5.2.2 Structural Biology Insights

  1. From a chemical perspective, explain why:
    • D39A and D35A mutations might be more disruptive experimentally than AF3 predicts
    • D39R and D35R mutations show large effects in both experiments and AF3 predictions
  2. What does this tell us about the different types of interactions that stabilize protein complexes?

5.2.3 Methodological Considerations

  1. What are the limitations of using AF3 for mutational studies? When might it be reliable vs unreliable?

  2. How could you validate these computational predictions experimentally?

  3. What other computational approaches could complement AF3 for studying mutation effects?

6 Advanced Analysis (Optional)

For students interested in deeper analysis:

  • Analyze specific interface regions in detail
  • Compare hydrogen bonding patterns between structures
  • Examine conformational changes upon mutation

6.2 Additional mutations

  • Predict effects of single mutations (D39A only, D35A only)
  • Test conservative mutations (D39E, D35E)
  • Explore mutations in Barnase instead of Barstar
Key Takeaways
  • AF3 excels at predicting wild-type protein complex structures with high confidence
  • Mutation effects are not always accurately captured, especially for subtle changes
  • Charge-reversal mutations (D→R) are better predicted than loss-of-function mutations (D→A)
  • Confidence metrics (pTM, ipTM) provide valuable information about prediction reliability
  • Experimental validation remains essential for understanding mutation effects
  • AI predictions should be interpreted alongside structural and chemical principles

6.3 Biological Significance

This analysis demonstrates that:

  1. Protein-protein interfaces rely on multiple, subtle interactions that may not be fully captured by current AI models

  2. Electrostatic interactions are crucial for the Barnase-Barstar interface and are better predicted than hydrophobic or entropic effects

  3. AF3 represents a powerful tool for structural biology but has limitations that must be understood

  4. Computational and experimental approaches are complementary for understanding protein function

The Barnase-Barstar system serves as an excellent model for understanding both the capabilities and limitations of AI-based structure prediction in the context of protein evolution and design.
